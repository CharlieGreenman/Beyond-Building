# CHAPTER SEVEN: CHILDREN IN THE POST-PURPOSE WORLD

### *What we owe the first generation that will never need to justify its existence by its usefulness*

Every parent lies to their children. Not cruelly, not even consciously most of the time — but structurally. The lie is woven into the entire enterprise of modern child-rearing, and it runs something like this:

*Work hard. Develop your skills. Build your strengths. Find something you're good at and get better at it. The world will reward you for your excellence.*

This lie has enough truth in it that it has survived for generations. Children who work hard often do achieve more than children who don't. Skills do matter. Excellence is real and often does get recognized. The lie is not a complete fabrication — it is a partial truth that has been allowed to carry too much moral weight.

What the lie conceals is the premise on which all of it depends: that human excellence, in a given domain, is something the world cannot easily replace. That if you become good enough at something — programming, designing, analyzing, healing, teaching — your goodness at it will constitute a form of security. A reason to be valued. A ground for self-worth.

ASI removes this premise at the root.

And this means that a generation of children being raised on the standard modern framework for child development is being prepared for a world that will not exist by the time they reach it. We are training them for a game that will have ended before they arrive at the field.

This is not a small problem. It is the most urgent practical implication of everything this book has been arguing. Because you can tell adults that their meaning framework needs reconstruction, and adults can, with effort and support, do the work of reconstructing it. Children don't need reconstruction. They need a different foundation from the beginning.

What does that foundation look like?

---

## The Current Framework

The contemporary approach to child development, across most cultures and economic classes that have moved beyond subsistence, is organized around a fairly coherent set of goals:

Cognitive development: language, mathematics, scientific reasoning, analytical skills — the mental tools that allow a person to process information and solve problems.

Skill development: not just general cognitive capacity but domain-specific excellence — athletic performance, musical facility, artistic technique, technical proficiency.

Character development: honesty, resilience, work ethic, social skill — the traits that allow a person to function effectively and be liked in the social and professional world.

Credentialing: the formal recognition of cognitive and skill development through grades, degrees, certifications, and institutional affiliations.

All of this is in service of a single underlying goal that no one states explicitly because it is so obvious it does not need stating: *preparing the child to be a competent, contributing adult*.

The framework is instrumentally coherent. The problem is that the instrument it's preparing children for — the contribution economy — is the thing being dismantled.

Imagine spending eighteen years training a child to be an extraordinary telegraph operator. The training itself might develop genuinely admirable qualities — patience, precision, the capacity to concentrate. But the context that gives the training meaning would be gone before the child could apply it. The specific orientation of all that effort would be, at best, beside the point.

This is what we are doing now. Not with telegraph operators, but with the entire category of human cognitive labor.

And here is the danger that follows: when the old framework collapses, the vacuum does not stay empty. Something fills it. And the thing most likely to fill it — because it requires no philosophical work, no interior reconstruction, no confrontation with hard questions about meaning — is appearance.

When cognitive credentials lose their power to differentiate, the easiest pivot is to the one form of status signaling that ASI cannot directly outperform: how you look. The orthodontics, the athletic physique, the curated personal brand, the right clothes, the careful grooming — these already function as an unacknowledged credential in every institution that officially evaluates only skill and character. Parents already invest heavily in their children's appearance alongside their education. The risk is that when education's promise breaks, the investment in appearance doesn't decrease. It intensifies. It becomes the thing that's left.

This would be the achievement-identity trap in a new costume. Still externally validated — you are as valuable as others judge you to be. Still contingent — beauty fades, fashion shifts, the standard changes. Still entirely missing the point. A generation that replaces "my worth is what I can do" with "my worth is how I look" has not escaped the crisis. It has merely traded one collapsing foundation for another that was never sound to begin with.

The alternative framework we need for children must be robust enough to resist this gravitational pull — not just the pull of obsolete credentialism, but the pull of whatever superficial replacement rushes in to take its place.

## What Education Was Really For

Before we can imagine a different framework, we need to understand what education, at its best, has actually been trying to do — underneath the instrumental layer.

Socrates, who initiated the Western philosophical tradition of education, was not trying to prepare his students for careers. He was trying to produce people who could live examined lives. The process he used was not instruction but inquiry — the famous elenchus, the relentless questioning that stripped away false certainties and left the student confronting the actual state of their knowledge. This process was painful. Socrates' interlocutors regularly ended conversations feeling worse than when they began, having discovered that they didn't know what they thought they knew.

The point was not the discomfort. The point was the *honest encounter with reality* that the discomfort produced. A person who had been through serious Socratic inquiry was a person who knew the difference between genuine understanding and performed confidence.

The liberal arts tradition that followed — however corrupted by credentialism it later became — shared this orientation. The study of history, philosophy, literature, mathematics, and science was not, in its original conception, vocational training in disguise. It was a project of *expanding the student's encounter with human experience* — with the range of things humans have thought, felt, built, destroyed, understood, and failed to understand. A student who had genuinely encountered Homer and Euclid and Aquinas and Newton and Shakespeare was a student with more *dimensions of experience available to them* — more ways of being alive, more angles from which to understand themselves and others, more capacity to find genuine depth in a given domain.

This is what education was for. We lost the thread of it somewhere between the GI Bill and the student loan crisis, as the credential arms race transformed universities from places of formation into expensive professional licensing systems.

But the original impulse was right. And it is exactly what we need to recover.

## An Education for Flourishing

What would it look like to educate children specifically for depth, presence, relation, and narrative — the four pillars of human flourishing in the post-ASI world?

**Teaching depth.** The current educational model is designed for breadth — exposure to many subjects, none in sufficient depth to produce the particular quality of experience that comes from genuine mastery. A student learns some biology, some history, some literature, some math, at a level that allows them to pass tests and check boxes.

An education organized around depth would give students extended time with fewer subjects. Not a semester of chemistry but three years of chemistry, deep enough to begin encountering the genuine questions the subject raises, the genuine beauty and strangeness of the molecular world. Not a unit on poetry but years of poetry — reading it, writing it, talking about it with someone who loves it, until the student begins to develop their own interior relationship with language.

The goal is not that every child becomes a chemist or a poet. The goal is that every child experiences what it feels like to go genuinely deep into something — to feel the texture of a domain that resists superficial engagement, and to discover that the resistance is part of what makes the engagement valuable.

**Teaching presence.** Attention practices. Silence. The capacity to be still with one's own experience without immediately reaching for distraction.

This is not meditation as ideology. It is attention as skill — like any other skill, developed through practice. Schools that have introduced structured attention practices report significant improvements in students' capacity for sustained focus, emotional regulation, and engagement with learning. The improvements are not mystical. They are neurological. The practice builds the neural infrastructure for presence.

More broadly, an education in presence means an education that takes *slow experience* seriously. The school visit to a forest where students are asked to spend an hour attending to a single square meter of ground. The music class that spends a month on a single piece of music, listening to it more and more carefully rather than surveying the whole canon. The writing class that focuses not on producing more words but on attending more carefully to the ones you have.

**Teaching relation.** Teaching relation means teaching children how to be genuinely present to another person — how to listen without immediately evaluating, how to disagree without contempt, how to be changed by encounter with a perspective genuinely different from their own.

This means classrooms organized around genuine collaborative inquiry — not "group projects" where students divide and conquer a task, but sustained engagement with genuinely hard questions where the diversity of perspectives is not a complication to manage but the entire point.

**Teaching narrative.** Children are natural storytellers and natural story-receivers. The educational task is not to introduce them to narrative but to develop their capacity to *inhabit their own story consciously* — to understand that the challenges they face are not interruptions of their education but the substance of it, to see their own lives as something worth attending to and eventually worth telling.

This means giving children genuine practice in autobiography — not in the journaling-as-assignment sense, but in the deeper sense of learning to witness their own experience. What happened? What did I feel? What did I understand that I didn't before? What is being asked of me by this difficulty?

## The Conversation We Need to Have with Children

There is one thing harder than redesigning educational institutions. It is being honest with children about why.

The current generation of children being raised in thoughtful, educated households is being raised with implicit promises. Work hard. Be good at things. The world will reward your excellence. These promises are well-intentioned. They are also, increasingly, not true in the way they used to be.

We owe children a different conversation. Not a despairing one. But an honest one.

Something like: *The world is changing in ways that make it more important than ever to develop as a person rather than just as a producer. The skills you build matter — but what matters more is who you become while you're building them. Go deep because depth is good, not because it will be rewarded. Be present because life is happening now, not because presence will get you a job. Love people because they are worth loving, not because relationships are strategically valuable.*

This is a conversation that requires parents to have done some of their own philosophical work first. You cannot transmit a meaning framework you have not lived. You cannot teach a child that depth is intrinsically valuable if your own life is organized around instrumental achievement.

The generational transmission of a new meaning framework begins not with curriculum reform. It begins with parents and teachers doing their own reconstruction — confronting their own productivity-based self-understanding and finding what lies beneath it.

The children who will come of age in the first decades of the ASI world deserve to inherit something more honest and more durable than the meaning framework that is currently being handed to them. They deserve parents and teachers and institutions that have done the work of figuring out what actually matters and are willing to say so.
