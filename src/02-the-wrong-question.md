# CHAPTER TWO: THE WRONG QUESTION

### *Why "what will humans do?" is the problem, not the solution*

We keep asking the wrong question.

"What will humans *do* after ASI?" "What role will humans *play*?" "What value will humans *add*?" "What will humans be *for*?"

Every one of these questions is structured the same way. They treat the human as an instrument and ask what the instrument's function will be in the new environment. They assume that the justification for human existence is something that can be *done*, something that *produces a result*, something that can be evaluated as useful or useless by some external measure.

This is the productivism assumption, and it is so embedded in our cognition that it feels like logic itself.

It is not logic. It is ideology. And it is approximately ten thousand years old.

Before agriculture, humans did not "have jobs." They had *lives*. They hunted, gathered, told stories, raised children, buried their dead, painted on cave walls, looked at stars. These activities were not instrumentally justified by their contribution to a larger productive system. They were what a human life *consisted of*. The question "but what are you contributing?" would have been unintelligible.

Agriculture changed everything. For the first time, human activity could be made legible in terms of surplus — what you produced beyond what you consumed. The surplus is what built civilizations. The surplus is what built armies, temples, libraries, universities, hospitals. The surplus framework produced genuinely extraordinary things.

But it also restructured the deep logic of human worth. Once it became possible to evaluate a person's contribution in terms of surplus production, it became possible — inevitable — to ask whether any particular person was *worth* their consumption. Whether their existence *justified its costs*.

Modern capitalism perfected this logic. Your salary is a measure of your marginal productivity. Your promotions track your surplus generation. Your sense of self-worth mirrors your market value. The most successful people in the world are those who can claim, with evidence, to have added enormous value to the productive system.

ASI makes every human's marginal productivity approximately zero.

If we keep asking "what will humans *do*?" we will keep arriving at despair, because the honest answer is: *nothing that a machine cannot do better*. That answer is only despairing, however, if we accept the premise that doing things — and doing them well — is what gives human life its value.

The right question is different.

The right question is: **What is a human being, and what does it mean for a human being to flourish?**

This question has been asked before. It is, in fact, the oldest question in philosophy. But we stopped taking it seriously once the surplus framework gave us a simpler answer. We collapsed "flourishing" into "productivity" and called it civilization.

Now productivity is being removed from the equation. And the ancient question returns, urgent and unavoidable, stripped of the comfortable shortcut we've been using for millennia.

What *is* a human being?

## Consciousness as the Only Non-Fungible Resource

Here is what I have come to believe, after years of circling this problem:

The only thing that ASI cannot replicate is not *what humans produce*. It is the fact that *there is something it is like to be a human*.

This phrase — "there is something it is like to be X" — is the philosopher Thomas Nagel's formulation of consciousness. A system is conscious, in this sense, if there is a subjective experience of being that system. There is something it is like to be a bat. There is something it is like to be in pain. There is something it is like to see red.

We do not know, and may never know with certainty, whether there is something it is like to be an ASI. The question of machine consciousness is genuinely open. But we know this: whatever the inner life of an ASI may be, it is not a *human* inner life. It has not been born. It has not faced death. It has not loved someone and been afraid of losing them. It has not experienced the specific weight of a human body in the specific stream of human time. It has not done something it cannot undo, and then had to keep living inside the self that did it. It has not waited for a diagnosis — not the abstract probability of an adverse outcome, but the actual sitting in an actual chair while an actual doctor chooses their words. It has not been a child who needed someone who did not come. It has not been lonely in a room full of people who were supposed to know it. It has not watched its own face change in a mirror over decades and felt, not calculated, the passage of its one life.

The human experience is not valuable because it produces good outputs. It is valuable because *it is happening*. Because somewhere, a conscious being is encountering the world — with all its beauty and horror and confusion and grace — and that encounter is *occurring*. That occurrence is, as far as we know, the only thing in the universe that has intrinsic value. Not because it's useful. Because it's *real in a way that matters*.

This is not mysticism. It is the oldest insight of every wisdom tradition on earth, dressed in contemporary language.

The Buddhist concept of *dukkha* (suffering/unsatisfactoriness) and the path to its relief — this is not about how to be more productive. It is about the quality of a conscious being's encounter with existence. The Stoic philosophers were not teaching a productivity system. They were teaching a way of inhabiting experience. Aristotle's *eudaimonia* — often translated "happiness" but better rendered "flourishing" — was not about output. It was about the full actualization of human capacities in the *living of a life*.

We abandoned these frameworks not because they were wrong but because the surplus economy made them seem beside the point. If you can measure productivity, why wrestle with the unquantifiable?

ASI removes the productivity metric. The unquantifiable is, suddenly, all that's left.

And that is not a tragedy. That is a return to something we should never have left.
