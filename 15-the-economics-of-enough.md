# CHAPTER FIFTEEN: THE ECONOMICS OF ENOUGH

### *How a civilization funds what it cannot measure without destroying it by measuring*

A market square, twenty years into the transition.

The buildings are older than the economy they now serve — repurposed storefronts, a former bank that is now a workshop, a library that has doubled in size. The market itself is open twice a week. A baker named Thomas sets out fourteen loaves of bread. He has been baking for twenty years — not because he needed the income, which has been unnecessary for most of that time, but because somewhere around year twelve he understood what bread could be. Not as fuel, not as commodity, but as a medium: flour, water, salt, heat, time, and the particular quality of attention that transforms these into something that nourishes in ways the ingredients do not explain.

A woman picks up a loaf, turns it over, sets it down. Picks up a different one. She does not pay in the way we understand payment. The exchange that governs this market is something between gift and barter — a system that emerged not from economic theory but from the practical necessity of people who have enough and want to share what they have made.

Thomas does not sell bread. He makes bread, and people take it, and in return they bring what they have made or offer what they can do. The man who made Thomas's oven did not charge for it. A girl who sweeps the market floor is not employed — she likes Thomas, she likes bread, and she is learning to bake.

This is not an economy in the sense we currently use the word. It is what an economy looks like when the problem of scarcity has been solved and the problem of meaning has replaced it.

## The Surplus Problem

A government committee sits around a table. Before them is a request for funding: a small group of mathematicians wants to spend seven years exploring "contemplative mathematics" — the intersections of mathematical structure with meditative practice and phenomenological inquiry. They cannot describe what they will produce. They cannot promise results. They can only say that the inquiry feels genuine and deep and that they need support to pursue it.

The committee's problem is this: they have the money. The surplus generated by ASI is enormous — far larger than any civilization has ever managed. The question is not whether they can fund this. The question is how to fund it without destroying it.

This is what I call the **metrics trap**, and it is productivism's last stronghold.

The metrics trap works as follows. To distribute public resources responsibly, you need criteria. Criteria require measurement. Measurement requires defining what counts as success. And the act of defining success changes the activity being measured — because the people engaged in the activity begin, consciously or not, to optimize for the metric rather than for the thing the metric was supposed to track.

We already know this. Every teacher who has watched standardized testing distort their classroom knows it. Every researcher who has watched impact factors distort their field knows it. Every artist who has watched audience metrics distort their work knows it. Goodhart's Law — that a measure which becomes a target ceases to be a good measure — is not a curiosity. It is a description of what happens every time we try to manage what we cannot quantify.

The things that matter most in human flourishing — depth of engagement, quality of presence, genuineness of relation, coherence of narrative — are precisely the things that resist measurement. Not because we lack sophisticated enough instruments but because measurement itself alters the relationship between the person and the activity. The mathematician who must demonstrate contemplative breakthroughs on a quarterly report is no longer doing contemplative mathematics. She is doing funded performance.

I argued in Chapter Two that the productive contribution framework collapses under ASI because human marginal productivity approaches zero. But the framework's deepest assumption — that value must be measurable to be real — survives the collapse of the framework itself. The metrics trap is what happens when a post-scarcity civilization tries to fund flourishing using the logic of scarcity. You end up measuring depth, which is like measuring love — the instrument touches the thing and the thing flinches away.

The committee, if it is wise, funds the contemplative mathematicians without requiring measurable outcomes. It funds them on trust.

## What "Work" Becomes

A man is building a stone wall. Nobody commissioned it. Nobody will pay for it. It serves no purpose except to mark the boundary between a meadow and a path, and even that function is not strictly necessary — the boundary was clear enough without it.

He is building it because the work requires a quality of attention he cannot find elsewhere. Each stone must be read — its weight, its shape, the way it wants to sit in relation to the stones around it. A good stone wall is not assembled. It is discovered — the builder finding, through patient attention, the arrangement that was latent in the materials all along.

He used to be an engineer. He built things that millions of people used. Now he builds a wall that nobody needs. He has never been more engaged with his work.

This is what "work" becomes when economic necessity is removed: not idleness, but the peculiar and intense form of engagement that emerges when you work because the work itself requires something of you.

I see three categories of what work becomes in this transition.

**Maintenance work as practice.** The basic labor of keeping a community, a home, a piece of land in good order. Cooking, cleaning, building, repairing, growing. This work has always been the most honest form of human activity — direct, embodied, yielding to immediate observation whether it has been done well or poorly. In a post-scarcity world, maintenance work is not the drudgery that must be endured to get to the real activities. It is a practice — a daily engagement with the physical world that cultivates the same attention the contemplatives cultivated in their cells.

**Depth work.** The sustained engagement with a domain — mathematics, music, ecology, philosophy, craft — that produces not output but transformation. This is the work of the Schools of Depth, described in the previous chapter. It is "work" in the sense that it requires discipline, effort, and the willingness to face difficulty. It is not work in the sense that it produces something for someone else's use.

**Gift work.** The bread Thomas bakes. The bowls the ceramicist throws. The wall the former engineer builds. Work done not for exchange but because the maker has gone deep enough into a practice to produce something worth sharing and wants to share it. Gift work is the natural expression of depth — the point at which private mastery becomes public generosity.

These categories are not rigid, and most people will move among them. The important thing is that none of them requires the framework of employment to be intelligible. I argued in Chapter Six that the identity crisis of the ASI transition is fundamentally a crisis of self-concept — of who you are when what you do no longer defines you. The answer is not to find new employment but to discover a different relationship to work itself: one grounded in engagement rather than compensation, in attention rather than ambition, in the quality of the doing rather than the value of the done.

## The Gift and the Trust

A room. Ten people around a table. They are trustees of what the community calls "the commons fund" — a pool of resources drawn from the civilization's ASI-generated surplus and distributed to individuals and small groups pursuing depth.

A request is before them. A woman wants to spend three years studying the migratory patterns of a particular species of bird — not to produce a paper or a documentary but because the birds have become her question. She has been watching them for a decade, first casually, then with increasing seriousness. She knows their routes, their habits, their silences. She wants three years of sustained attention to understand something she cannot yet articulate.

The trustees evaluate this request by a single criterion: **genuine depth of engagement.** Not productivity, not potential impact, not measurable outcomes. They ask whether this person is genuinely deep in the inquiry — whether the work has the quality of commitment and attention that distinguishes depth from hobby, obsession, or avoidance.

This is trust-based funding, and it has precedents. The MacArthur Fellowship awards unrestricted grants to individuals who have demonstrated exceptional depth in their fields, with no requirement to produce specific outcomes. Monastic endowments sustained communities of inquiry for centuries on the principle that the value of contemplative life could not be measured but should be funded anyway. Indigenous gift economies distributed resources on the basis of relationship and demonstrated commitment rather than contractual obligation.

The model I envision is a nested structure of trusts: local trusts funded by the community, regional trusts funded by the civilization's surplus, and a planetary trust that supports the deepest and most sustained forms of human inquiry. Each level operates on the same principle — that depth of engagement is recognizable even when it is not measurable, and that the people closest to the work are the best judges of its genuineness.

The obvious objection is free-riding. If resources are distributed on trust, what prevents people from claiming depth they do not possess? What prevents the system from being gamed by those who have mastered the language of contemplative aspiration without the substance?

I want to be honest about this: the risk is real. Any system that distributes resources on trust will sometimes be exploited. The question is whether the cost of exploitation exceeds the cost of the alternative — which is the metrics trap, the slow destruction of the thing you are trying to fund by the act of measuring it.

The answer, I believe, is that small-scale trust works. A community of two hundred people can recognize the difference between genuine depth and performed depth because they live alongside the person and see the work over time. A trust committee that knows its applicants — that has watched them struggle, fail, persist, and grow — can make judgments that no metric can capture. The system fails when it scales beyond relationship. But it does not need to scale beyond relationship. It needs to be nested: small circles of trust within larger circles of trust, each level accountable to the one below it and funded by the one above it.

This is not utopian. It is how scholarship worked for most of human history — patronage, mentorship, community support, and the judgment of people who had the wisdom to recognize genuine pursuit. What is new is the scale of the surplus that makes it possible, and the philosophical commitment that justifies it.

## Enough

A woman on a porch. It is evening. She has a cup of tea, a book, a view of hills that she has been watching change with the seasons for eleven years. She has enough.

Not enough in the ascetic sense — she is not deprived. Her home is warm, her food is good, her health is attended to, her community is close. Not enough in the sense of settling — she has a practice, a deep engagement with botanical illustration, that will never be finished. She is not idle. She is not bored.

Enough in the philosophical sense: the recognition that the relationship between material conditions and human flourishing is not linear. More is not better past a certain point. That point — which economists have tried to quantify and which varies between individuals but is far lower than developed-world consumption levels — is where sufficiency begins. Beyond it, additional material resources do not increase the capacity for depth, presence, relation, or narrative. They often decrease it, by introducing complexity, distraction, and the particular anxiety of having more than you can attend to.

"Enough" is not austerity. I want to be careful about this, because the romanticization of poverty — the idea that material simplicity is inherently virtuous — has a long and damaging history. Poverty is not ennobling. It is a form of suffering-as-obstacle, in the distinction I drew in Chapter Nine: suffering that prevents engagement with life rather than deepening it. The first obligation of a post-scarcity civilization is to eliminate poverty entirely, to ensure that no human being lacks the material conditions for flourishing. This is not optional. It is the foundation on which everything else is built.

But once that foundation is in place, the question becomes: what relationship to material abundance actually supports a deeply lived life? And the honest answer, confirmed by every wisdom tradition and by the emerging science of well-being, is that the answer is not "more."

The woman on the porch is not opposed to innovation. She uses tools and technologies that make her work richer and her life more comfortable. Innovation continues in this civilization — but it moves in the direction of depth rather than scale. A better tool for botanical illustration. A building material that ages more gracefully. A method of food preservation that enhances flavor. Innovation in the service of quality rather than quantity, of depth rather than breadth, of enough rather than more.

This is the economics of enough. Not an economics of austerity but an economics of sufficiency — a system organized around the principle that the surplus exists to fund human flourishing, that flourishing requires enough and is damaged by excess, and that the hardest economic discipline is not producing more but distributing wisely what has already been produced.

I argued in Chapter Nine that sufficiency requires a willingness to accept discomfort — the discomfort of not optimizing, of leaving surplus on the table, of choosing depth over scale. This is the economic expression of the same argument. A civilization that chooses enough over more is making the same choice that an individual makes when they choose presence over productivity: the harder path, the less measurable outcome, the thing that cannot be justified instrumentally but that anyone who has lived it recognizes as the better life.

---

The market is closing. Thomas wraps the last loaf. A child has been watching him all morning — standing at a distance, studying his hands, the way children study things they want to understand.

"Can I learn?" the child asks.

Thomas looks at her. He sees in her the particular quality of attention that cannot be taught but can be recognized and cultivated.

"Come tomorrow. Four o'clock. Bring your hands."

She nods and walks away. The loaf in its wrapping goes with her — Thomas pressed it into her hands as she turned. The exchange is complete: bread offered, apprenticeship requested, something beginning that neither of them can yet see the shape of.

This is the economics of enough. Not a system. A way of being with each other that becomes possible when the question shifts from "how much can we produce?" to "how well can we live?"
