# CHAPTER EIGHT: NEW INSTITUTIONS FOR HUMAN FLOURISHING

### *What needs to be built when the purpose of building has changed*

Civilizations encode their values in institutions. Not in their stated values — those are the things inscribed on monuments and spoken at commencements. In their *actual* institutions — the structures they build and fund and maintain, the ones they cannot imagine getting along without.

Look at what we have built. Universities that credential professional competence. Corporations that organize productive activity. Hospitals that extend productive years. Financial systems that measure and store the surplus of productive work. Courts that protect the property produced by productive work. Militaries that defend the geopolitical arrangements that make productive work possible.

Every major institution of modern civilization is in service of the productive contribution economy. Every one of them is structured around the assumption that the central human project is the organized transformation of resources into value — and that supporting this transformation is what institutions are for.

What institutions would a civilization build if its central project were human flourishing rather than productivity?

The question is not hypothetical. We are going to need to answer it. And building institutions is slow work — slow enough that we need to begin imagining and prototyping them now, before the transition makes the need undeniable.

---

## What Institutions Do

Before imagining new institutions, it is worth being precise about what institutions actually do — what makes them necessary rather than optional.

Institutions do three things that individuals and informal groups cannot do reliably or at scale.

They **encode and transmit knowledge and practice** across generations. The university transmits scientific knowledge. The medical profession transmits clinical knowledge. The legal system transmits accumulated wisdom about social coordination. Without institutions, knowledge has to be rediscovered by each generation. With institutions, it accumulates.

They **create the conditions for demanding practices** to be sustained. A contemplative monastery does not merely house monks — it creates the schedule, the community, the accountability, and the material support that make a serious contemplative life possible in ways it would not be if each practitioner were on their own. The institution holds the practice.

They **signal what a civilization takes seriously**. The existence of a great university signals that a civilization values knowledge. The existence of a well-funded national health system signals that a civilization values health. Institutions are where rhetoric meets reality — where you can see what a civilization actually thinks matters.

By these criteria, we have almost no institutions designed for human flourishing. We have many for human productivity. We have some, in the form of churches and monasteries, that were historically oriented toward something closer to flourishing — but these have been marginalized in the modern world, and many have been corrupted by the same productivity logic they were supposed to transcend.

We need new institutions.

## Schools of Depth

Not universities. Not schools in the current sense. Something new — institutions organized specifically around the development of depth in a given domain, with no credential attached and no career trajectory implied.

The closest existing models: the Japanese atelier, where a student spends years or decades with a single master, going progressively deeper into a single domain. The conservatory, where a musician studies their instrument not primarily to perform professionally but to develop the quality of musicianship that makes serious music possible. The Socratic school — a community of inquiry organized around hard questions, sustained over time.

In the post-ASI world, where no credential has economic value and no professional career in cognitive labor is secure, this model could expand to encompass any domain of genuine depth. Mathematics not as job preparation but as an encounter with the strange beauty of abstract structure. History not as information download but as a long investigation of how humans have organized their lives and what they have made of their circumstances. Literature as a practice of deep reading — reading slowly, reading carefully, reading with one text in conversation with another across centuries.

These institutions would be organized around the experience of the students, not the productivity of the output. Their success metric would not be employment outcomes or research publications. It would be something much harder to measure and much more important: the quality of engagement, the development of depth, the cultivation of a relationship with a domain that transforms the person who enters it.

A natural question: without credentials or career outcomes as quality signals, how would these institutions maintain rigor? Who teaches, and by what authority?

The answer is embedded in the model itself. The master-apprentice relationship does not need external validation because its quality is immediately apparent to everyone involved. A student who has spent three years studying mathematics with a genuine mathematician knows whether the encounter has been real — whether the teacher sees what they see, whether the questions are getting harder and more interesting, whether the domain is opening up. The credential economy needed external metrics because it scaled education beyond the range of genuine relationship. The school of depth works precisely because it does not scale in the industrial sense — because its quality depends on the same thing quality has always depended on: the presence of someone who has gone deep and can recognize depth in another.

This sounds utopian. It is not. It is the oldest model of education, predating the credential economy by millennia. We are rediscovering it because the credential economy is ending.

## New Monasteries

I use the word deliberately, knowing it will put some readers off. I mean something specific by it, and I do not mean it in a narrowly religious sense.

A monastery, at its structural core, is a community organized around a shared practice and a shared understanding of what constitutes a good life. The practice generates the community; the community sustains the practice; the shared understanding of the good life gives both their direction.

The Christian monastic tradition produced, over centuries, some of the most sophisticated institutions for contemplative development ever built. The Rule of Benedict — written in the sixth century as a guide for monastic communities — contains more practical wisdom about how to structure a community for human flourishing than almost any document produced since. Not because of its theology, which is specific to its tradition, but because of its attention to the basic conditions that make a communal life of depth and presence possible: rhythm, accountability, simplicity, hospitality, shared work, shared practice.

What would a secular monastery look like — or more precisely, a monastery organized around practices that can be held across different metaphysical commitments?

A community of perhaps thirty to two hundred people, organized around a shared daily rhythm that includes periods of silence and contemplative practice, shared work in service of the community itself, communal meals, ongoing inquiry into the questions that animate the community's shared life, and regular time for members to pursue the domain of depth they have chosen.

The shared metaphysical commitments are minimal: that human consciousness matters, that attention can be cultivated, that a life lived with depth and presence is qualitatively better than a life lived on default settings, and that community is necessary for sustaining serious practice.

The domain-specific practices can vary widely: one community might be organized around a specific contemplative tradition; another around artistic practice; another around philosophical inquiry; another around a particular engagement with the natural world. What they share is the structural commitment to depth, the communal holding of serious practice, and the understanding that the good life is not the productive life.

The dangers are real and worth naming. Any institution that organizes a community around shared values and shared practice can become a cult — an insular group that confuses its particular practices with universal truth and punishes dissent. The history of intentional communities is littered with examples: charismatic leaders who exploited the vulnerability that genuine community requires, ideological rigidity that crushed the openness it claimed to cultivate, insularity that made the community a refuge from the world rather than a different way of engaging with it.

The structural safeguards against these failures are known, if not always implemented: pluralism of leadership, transparent governance, regular engagement with the wider world, explicit norms of intellectual openness, and — perhaps most importantly — a culture that welcomes departure. A community you cannot leave is not a community. It is a prison with philosophical décor.

These communities exist in embryonic form already — in intentional communities, in retreat centers, in certain academic departments that have retained the spirit of genuine inquiry. What they lack is scale, legitimacy, and the material support to be genuine alternatives to the productivity economy rather than expensive weekend experiences for the already wealthy.

## Institutions of Relation

The most underbuilt category of institution in the modern world is one that supports genuine human connection.

We have social media, which simulates the experience of connection while delivering something closer to its opposite — performance, comparison, and the hijacking of the neural systems that evolved for social bonding. We have therapy, which does real work but is individual, expensive, and stigmatized. We have religious congregations, which at their best provide real community but which are declining rapidly in the developed world.

What we almost entirely lack are secular institutions organized specifically around the practice of genuine relation — institutions designed to support the experience of being known, being witnessed, being challenged by genuine encounter with other people.

The closest existing models: the twelve-step community, which despite its theological framing has produced extraordinary depth of relation in its meetings — honest, non-hierarchical, organized around shared vulnerability and mutual support. The Quaker meeting, with its practice of communal silence and the expectation that speech will emerge from genuine experience rather than performance. The philosophical café tradition, where sustained conversation about hard questions creates unexpected depth of encounter.

What these share is a structure that creates the conditions for honest encounter rather than performance. Practices that require vulnerability. Expectations of honesty. The absence of a productivity agenda — nothing here needs to be accomplished, nothing needs to be decided, nothing needs to be useful. The conversation is the point.

At scale, what we need is something like neighborhood-level institutions of relation — accessible, ongoing communities of encounter, organized around the practice of knowing and being known. Not support groups for specific problems. Not networking events in disguise. Communities of mutual presence, where people come together regularly to actually encounter each other.

What would this look like concretely? A physical space in a neighborhood — not a bar, not a community center in the current sense, but something designed specifically for the practice of encounter. Regular gatherings — weekly, perhaps — with a structure borrowed from the traditions that have done this well: a period of silence to arrive, a practice of honest sharing without cross-talk, a conversation organized around a real question rather than an agenda. No membership fees that would exclude. No professional facilitators who would medicalize it. A practice held by the community itself, with enough structure to create safety and enough openness to allow surprise.

The digital question is worth addressing directly. Online communities have proven capable of extraordinary things — real mutual support, sustained intellectual inquiry, the creation of shared culture across distances. They have also proven capable of spectacular failure — the simulation of community without its substance, the replacement of encounter with performance and surveillance. The institutions of relation I am describing are not anti-digital. But they are rooted in physical presence, because the kind of encounter they cultivate requires the full bandwidth of embodied human interaction — the tone of voice, the silence that communicates as much as speech, the experience of being in a room with someone who is actually looking at you. Digital tools can extend and support these communities. They cannot replace the gathering itself.

## The Economics of Flourishing

None of this is possible without addressing the most practical question: in the post-ASI world, who does the work of economic survival, and how is its output distributed?

The standard argument for universal basic income is that it provides a floor of material security that allows people to pursue non-productive activities without starving. This is correct and necessary. But it is not sufficient.

The institutions described here — schools of depth, new monasteries, communities of relation — require not just that individuals have enough to survive but that communities have the material resources to sustain serious shared life.

Here is the argument for why this becomes politically possible: in the post-ASI world, productive labor — the labor that generates economic surplus — will be performed primarily by machines. The surplus will exist. The question is where it goes. In a world where human labor is no longer the primary source of value, the political argument for distributing that surplus to the humans who are no longer generating it must change. It cannot rest on the labor theory of value. It must rest on something else.

That something else is the argument of this book: that human flourishing has intrinsic value, that a civilization that supports it is better than one that doesn't, and that the surplus generated by ASI should be used to create the conditions for human beings to live well rather than accumulated by whoever happens to own the machines.

The mechanisms for this distribution are not mysterious. Public funding of flourishing institutions — as natural an expenditure as public funding of roads or schools — is one path. Endowment models, where a portion of ASI-generated surplus is invested to permanently sustain institutions of depth and relation, is another. Community land trusts that give new monasteries the same material security that medieval endowments gave their predecessors. The details are for economists and policymakers to work out. The philosophical point is prior: the surplus must be understood as belonging, in some fundamental sense, to the civilization that generated it, not merely to the owners of the machines.

The transition will be messy. We will not move cleanly from one institutional landscape to another. For a period — perhaps a long one — the old institutions will coexist with the new. Universities will continue to grant credentials while schools of depth develop alongside them. Traditional corporations will continue to organize productive labor while new monasteries experiment with different forms of shared life. The goal is not to dismantle the old before the new is ready. It is to begin building the new now, so that as the old loses its grip, alternatives already exist.

## What We Build Reveals What We Believe

The institutions a civilization builds are its most honest autobiography. More reliable than its constitutions, more truthful than its speeches, more enduring than its art. You can read a civilization's actual values by looking at where it puts its resources, its talent, its sustained institutional attention — and ignoring everything it says about itself.

By this measure, our civilization believes in productivity. Fervently, comprehensively, with the full weight of its institutional imagination.

The institutions described in this chapter — schools of depth, new monasteries, communities of relation, and the economic structures to sustain them — are not proposals for a distant utopia. They are the first concrete steps toward a different autobiography. They can be prototyped now, in embryonic form, by people who recognize that the current institutional landscape was built for a world that is ending and that the world being born will need something different.

The work of building institutions is slow — slower than writing code, slower than shipping product, slower than any of the forms of building the productivity economy taught us to admire. It requires patience with ambiguity, tolerance for the messiness of human community, and the willingness to invest in something whose returns cannot be measured on a quarterly basis.

But it is the most consequential work a civilization can do. The institutions we build now will shape what human life looks like on the other side of the transition. They will determine whether the post-ASI world is one of languishing abundance or genuine flourishing — whether the surplus serves human depth or merely human comfort.

We built the institutions we have because we believed the central human project was production. If we come to believe the central human project is flourishing, we will need to build accordingly. And the building must start before the need is undeniable — because institutions, unlike software, cannot be shipped overnight.
